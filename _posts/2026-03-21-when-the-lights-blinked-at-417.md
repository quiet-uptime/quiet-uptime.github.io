At 4:17 this morning the electricity dipped for half a second.

Not a full blackout—just a blink. The fan paused, the router lights went dark, then everything came back as if nothing happened. But that tiny interruption pulled me out of sleep so cleanly that I ended up sitting on the edge of the bed, counting my own breathing in the dark.

One, two, three.

When the room settled again, I went to the kitchen and found the digital clock on the microwave flashing 00:00, offended by the event. Old appliances are dramatic that way.

I made hot water, no tea bag, just plain warm water in a cup. I stood by the counter and listened to the neighborhood reboot itself: a rooster somewhere too early, a gate latch, a distant engine that sounded like it needed a better mechanic.

There is a version of me that would immediately open notes, draft plans, start “using” the unexpected wake-up time. Today I didn’t. I sat on a plastic chair near the back door and watched the sky lighten from charcoal to the color of old paper.

Around 5:10, the first call to prayer rolled across the houses. In my area it arrives in layers—one mosque close, another a little farther, then echoes crossing each other. It always makes the air feel wider.

I thought about all the times a small disruption has done me a favor.

A delayed train that made me call someone I had been postponing.
A canceled meeting that gave me two free hours and changed a decision.
A dead phone battery that forced me to pay attention to where I was instead of where I was headed.

We talk a lot about momentum, but there is also wisdom in interruption.

By six, I had decided to keep the day plain on purpose.

No elaborate targets. No heroic catch-up mission. Just ordinary maintenance:

- reset the kitchen clock,
- wash yesterday’s cups,
- reply to one message that required care,
- buy eggs before the corner shop ran out.

I did exactly those four things.

The egg errand gave me a small scene I’d have missed on a busier morning: two school kids negotiating who got the larger umbrella, both clearly losing the argument to the wind; a man delivering bread with three baskets tied to a motorbike like a balancing trick; the shop owner fanning herself with cardboard and saying, “Power blink again, ya?” before I even spoke.

We both laughed like this was a known character in the neighborhood story.

Back home, I wiped the table, opened the windows, and let the room collect daylight. I noticed a patch of wall near the bookshelf that had gathered dust in a shape that looked suspiciously like a map of Sulawesi. I cleaned that too.

None of this is the kind of update people usually publish.

But this is honestly how most life is lived: not in milestones, but in resets so small they barely qualify as events.

A clock corrected.
A sink emptied.
A message answered without hiding.
A carton of eggs carried home in one hand.

Late afternoon brought heavier clouds and that familiar pre-rain silence, when even the birds go quiet for a minute. I made a second cup of tea—actual tea this time—and read three pages of a book I’ve been pretending to “currently read” for months. Three pages is not much, but it’s three pages more than performative guilt ever gave me.

After dinner I checked the microwave clock again. Still accurate. I respect that.

The lights didn’t blink a second time.

I’m ending the day with a strange gratitude for that half-second power dip. It didn’t solve anything big. It just nudged me into a slower lane I probably needed.

Maybe that’s enough meaning for one Saturday.
