This morning I arrived at the neighborhood warung five minutes before I actually needed to be there.

That never happens.

I am usually the person speed-walking the final block while mentally composing apologies to time itself. But today, somehow, I had five spare minutes and nowhere urgent to spend them.

So I stood by the glass fridge, looked at rows of bottled tea, and watched a very small slice of local civilization unfold.

A school kid in oversized shoes counted coins twice before buying iced tea.

An ojol driver asked for extra sambal and received a lecture about his stomach.

Someone’s toddler announced “MAMA ITU AYAM” at maximum volume while pointing at fried tofu.

A man in office clothes carefully selected one banana like he was choosing a witness.

Nothing major happened. It was perfect.

I think we overlook how grounding these tiny public moments can be. Not the curated social stuff—just shared everyday logistics. Waiting in line. Handing over exact change. Asking whether the eggs are fresh. Nodding at people you only know by routine.

There’s something stabilizing about doing ordinary things in places that don’t ask you to perform a version of yourself.

At the warung, no one needs your personal brand.

You either brought your own bag or you didn’t.

You either remembered your wallet or you’re doing the embarrassed QR scan dance.

You either know what you need for breakfast or you buy random things and call it improvisation.

(Respect to improvisation. It keeps half of us alive.)

Lately I’ve been trying to notice these micro-rituals as real parts of well-being, not background noise. We spend so much mental energy on big plans—quarterly goals, career arcs, life upgrades—that we sometimes forget a day can also be rescued by small predictable anchors.

Mine are not glamorous.

Boiling water before checking messages.

Buying fruit before I can talk myself out of buying fruit.

Putting keys in the same bowl every time.

Opening the same notebook page format when work starts.

It’s a modest architecture, but it holds.

People love to say “consistency is everything,” and usually they mean dramatic discipline. Wake at 5. Cold shower. 10,000 steps. Learn a language by noon. Build a startup by Tuesday.

I admire the energy, but my real life works better with softer consistency.

Take medicine on time.

Return borrowed things.

Check if there’s rice before dinner hour.

Refill the water before the gallon is completely empty.

Reply before silence turns into avoidance.

Do the little thing while it’s still little.

It’s not cinematic. It’s maintenance.

And maintenance is underrated because it doesn’t produce immediate applause.

Nobody claps when you finally throw away duplicate cables.

Nobody posts congratulations when your pantry makes sense.

Nobody gives awards for remembering to schedule the boring appointment you keep postponing.

But these acts remove friction from future-you’s life, and future-you is a real person with limited patience.

At the warung counter, I ended up buying tempeh, spinach, two limes, and one packet of instant coffee I absolutely did not need. The ibu behind the counter looked at me, looked at the coffee, and said, “Kamu tidur jam berapa sih?”

Fair question.

I laughed, paid, and walked home through a street already warming under the sun. Someone was washing a motorbike with impossible dedication. Two cats negotiated territorial law under a parked car. A delivery truck blocked half the road and nobody seemed shocked.

By the time I reached home, I felt better in a way that had nothing to do with productivity.

Just... more located.

More in my own life.

Maybe that’s what I’m trying to protect lately: a sense of being located. In my body, in my neighborhood, in my actual day—not only in plans for next week or tabs about things I might optimize someday.

Big goals still matter. Ambition still matters. But so does buying spinach before it sells out.

So does chatting with the same cashier who remembers your usual order.

So does knowing which corner gets sunlight at 8 a.m.

So does keeping enough gas in the motorbike that you don’t start your day with panic.

If today feels scattered, maybe don’t begin with reinvention. Begin with one concrete errand done calmly.

Walk to the nearest warung.

Buy what you actually need.

Say thank you.

Come home and put things where they belong.

Sometimes that’s enough structure for a day to click into place.
